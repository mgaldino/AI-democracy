# Introduction rewrite: AI Automation, Regime Type, and Crossed Fragility

**Original file**: /Users/manoelgaldino/Documents/DCP/Papers/IA-dem/paper.Rmd
**Date**: 2026-04-03

---

The political consequences of AI automation depend on timing. A technology that displaces workers immediately generates visible grievance and organized opposition. One that complements workers for years before replacing them generates false security and disoriented loss. We show that these two trajectories produce opposite fragility patterns across regime types: democracies are stable under rapid displacement but fragile under threshold automation, while autocracies exhibit the reverse.

Consider the United States and China adopting the same AI. In one scenario, factory automation displaces workers over months: protests build, unions organize, and the government responds with retraining programs. In the other, AI augments professional services until a threshold is crossed and entire job categories vanish. The first shock is legible; the second is a surprise. Whether the regime survives depends on whether it can act without a visible crisis and whether organized opposition degrades coercive capacity. Democracies require a visible crisis to mobilize compensation; autocracies require the absence of organized opposition to maintain repression.

The regime stability literature treats economic shocks as uniform events defined by magnitude (Acemoglu and Robinson 2006; Przeworski 2005). The automation literature distinguishes trajectories, comparing rapid displacement with threshold substitution (Gans and Goldfarb 2026; Autor, Levy, and Murnane 2003), but has not examined their political implications. No formal model connects the shape of economic disruption to regime-specific survival.

We build a two-period model in the Open Economy Politics tradition (Lake 2009), taking the economics of automation as given and deriving the political-institutional consequences. The model has one trajectory-varying primitive: signal noise ($\sigma$). Under rapid displacement, the approaching shock is visible and workers coordinate before it arrives. Under threshold automation, the complementary phase masks the coming substitution and coordination fails. This single informational parameter, processed through regime-specific institutional responses (compensation in democracies, repression in autocracies), generates the crossed fragility pattern as an equilibrium outcome.

Three results follow. First, the crossed pattern holds for autocracies with moderate repressive capacity (Proposition 3). Rapid shocks generate common knowledge of grievance (Chwe 2001), enabling collective action that democracies channel into compensation but that degrades autocratic repression (Kuran 1991; Edmond 2013). Threshold shocks produce strategic uncertainty that prevents coordination, paralyzing democratic response while preserving repressive effectiveness. Second, autocratic stability comes at a welfare cost equal to the repressive threshold: when autocracies survive, their citizens absorb the full automation loss (Proposition 4). Third, comprehensive social insurance functions as institutional insurance against delayed shocks (Propositions 5-6). Democracies with standing welfare states survive threshold automation through the same logic as autocracies with standing repressive capacity, with strictly superior welfare. The welfare gap equals exactly $\bar{\kappa}$.

This formalizes a specific implication of the class compromise tradition (Przeworski and Wallerstein 1982; Moene and Wallerstein 2001): when the democratic bargain is institutionalized as a standing welfare state, it insures the regime itself against technological disruption.

Section 2 develops a unified coordination model in which trajectory-dependent signal noise generates democratic compensation and autocratic repressive effectiveness as equilibrium outcomes. Section 3 derives Propositions 1-4 and comparative statics. Section 4 develops the welfare state extension (Propositions 5-7) and a five-type regime typology with empirical proxies. Section 5 discusses limitations. Two appendices provide fuller derivations of the democratic voting game and the coordination equilibrium.
