## 1. Introduction

Democracies are fragile under economic crises while autocracies prove resilient (Przeworski et al. 2000). No formal model explains why, or specifies when this pattern reverses. We formalize the conditions and show that comprehensive social insurance achieves the same institutional resilience as repressive capacity, without the human cost. The answer depends on the trajectory of the economic shock. When automation displaces workers rapidly and visibly, democracies are stable: observable distress triggers compensatory policy. Autocracies are vulnerable: organized opposition degrades repressive effectiveness. When automation initially complements labor and only later substitutes it, the pattern inverts. Democracies cannot respond to a shock they have not observed. Autocracies contain disoriented dissent with standing repressive apparatus.

The mechanism is worker coordination. Consider assembly-line workers displaced overnight by AI: shared, visible job loss generates common knowledge of grievance (Chwe 2001) and enables collective action. Now consider radiologists augmented by AI for years, then abruptly replaced once diagnostic accuracy crosses a threshold. Sudden displacement after apparent prosperity produces strategic uncertainty that impedes coordination. These two trajectories interact differently with how each regime stabilizes. Democracies compensate, but compensation requires a political signal, an observable crisis. Autocracies repress, but repression fails against organized, legitimate opposition (Kuran 1991; Edmond 2013). Rapid shocks provide the signal that democracies need and generate the organized opposition that autocracies cannot contain. Threshold shocks deny both.

The regime stability literature has not explored this interaction. Acemoglu and Robinson (2006) show that transitory shocks trigger regime transitions but treat transitoriness as binary. Boix (2003) links inequality to democratization without considering how the path to inequality matters. Przeworski (2005, 2006) models democracy as self-enforcing but is comparative-static in income. Svolik (2012) analyzes authoritarian stability as an agency problem between autocrat and elite; our model addresses a different margin, containment of affected workers. The empirical record documents the asymmetry (Przeworski et al. 2000), but its formal conditions remain unspecified.

We build a two-period model in the Open Economy Politics tradition (Lake 2009), importing the economic premise from the O-Ring automation framework (Gans and Goldfarb 2026; see also Autor, Levy, and Murnane 2003; Acemoglu and Restrepo 2020). The model produces three results. First, a crossed fragility pattern: for autocracies with moderate repressive capacity, democracies survive rapid shocks while autocracies do not, and autocracies survive threshold shocks while democracies do not (Proposition 3). Second, autocratic stability comes at a welfare cost equal to the repressive threshold: when autocracies survive, their citizens absorb the full shock (Proposition 4). Third, the welfare state functions as institutional insurance against delayed shocks, achieving the same resilience as repressive capacity while compensating affected workers instead of leaving them to bear the cost (Propositions 7-8). This last result connects to the class compromise tradition (Przeworski and Wallerstein 1982; Moene and Wallerstein 2001) and to models of authoritarian bargains (Desai, Olofsgard, and Yousef 2009).

Section 2 presents the model. Section 3 derives Propositions 1-4 and comparative statics. Section 4 develops the welfare state extension and a five-type regime typology with empirical proxies. Section 5 discusses limitations. Three appendices provide microfoundations and an extension to populist platform choice.
